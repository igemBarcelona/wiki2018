LONG CHAIN FATTY ACUD INTRACELLULAR BIOSENSOR

Analyzing fatty acid uptake capabilities of bacteria is not an easy task. Up to the moment, the most reliable technique is GC/MS. This method, only allows to obtain discrete data and it is very expensive. Also, very few laboratories have the necessary equipment. Therefore, performing these experiments is a task that can only be done by a very limited part of the research community.

Our team aims to design a genetic long chain fatty acid (LCFA) sensor device. This system will allow a reliable characterization of the fatty acid absorption capabilities of any bacterial system. This system can be coupled to other genes allowing their expression only in the presence of LCFA without compromising the LCFA uptake capabilities of bacteria. Thus creating a LCFA-dependent expression system.

In order to sense LCFAs, we take advantage of the E. coli endogenous promoter pFad. This promotor works as follows:

Figure 1 | The pfad promoter is constitutively repressed by FadR protein. LCFA enter the cell through the outer membrane receptor fadL, then, they get bound to acety-CoA. LCFA-acetyl-CoA is able to bind to FadR which allows it to be released from the promoter making the later active

FadR protein is the main player in transcription regulation of fatty acid (FA) metabolism. It is involved in negative regulation of FA degradation, thus constitutively repressing the Fad genes. This repression is mediated through the binding of LCFA-acetyl-CoA to FadR and the following release of FadR from the promoters of the Fad genes allowing its transcription.

In bacteria, this sensing device is coupled to the transcription of Fad genes. These genes code for the beta-oxidation enzymes, which are the ones that metabolize LCFA.

Other iGEM teams have previously attempted to create LCFA reporter systems by coupling the pFad promoter to a fluorescent protein, such as NTU_Taida 2014. However, their systems had a very high baseline expression of the reporter proteins. This did not allow them to see a significant signal rise after induction with LCFA. In order to lower the baseline expression, these teams overexpressed the repressor FadR. However, by overexpressing FadR, the Fad genes get repressed, this way their systems interfered with LCFA absorption and metabolism. This is a major concern as it diminishes a lot LCFA absorption and it does not allow to reliably discriminate within bacterial systems with enhanced LCFA uptake.

In order to tackle this problem, our team has designed a LCFA biosensor that does not need FadR overexpression to work. To do so, we have followed four different approaches to really be able to modulate the activation threshold and to diminish the baseline expression.

Use of different ribosome binding sites

Although the promoter has leakage, if the RBS is weak with low LCFA indices the signal will not be transmitted to the next node.

High-pass filter

Through an incoherent feed-forward loop the effect called high-pass filter occurs. This structure is widely used in the treatment of electrical and radiofrequency signals, since it causes the only signals that pass to be those of a high frequency. In our case, the highpass filter eliminates weak signals by passing only those strong, in other words, signals from higher LCFA concentrations. In this way we manage to eliminate the baseline that the promoter has in case.

Inducible LuxR-pLux engineered device

We coupled the pfadBA promoter to the LuxR/pluxR lactone inducible system. This way, although the baseline expression of LuxR is high, after LCFA induction there is a >10X folds concentration increase. This allows the differential expression of the reporter protein, when the system is induced by the correct concentration of lactone.
