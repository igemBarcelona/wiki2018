Enhancing E. Coli beta-oxidation

The main goal of our project is to obtain a system with an increased fatty acid (FA) uptake. In order to asses this, different approaches were considered. The first one was to overexpress the own E. coli fatty acids degradation family genes.

FAs are essential membrane components and important sources of metabolic energy. Moreover, they constitute important members of signalling pathways. Here we have been working with E. coli, one of the best studied models of FA metabolism. E. coli can use FAs with various chain lengths as sole carbon and energy sources. After uptake, these FAs can either be degraded via the beta-oxidation pathway or used as precursors for membrane phospholipid biosynthesis. The degradation pathway is catalysed by the enzymes encoded by the fad regulon, which includes FadL, FadD, FadE, FadA and FadB [1].

FadL and FadD are responsible for the FA transport across the cell membrane. FadL is an outer membrane homodimeric channel involved in the transport of long-chain fatty acids (LCFA) to de periplasm. From the periplasm to the cytosol, the transport is performed by FadD and is coupled to the acyl-CoA ester formation. To metabolize FAs, they must be activated to acyl- CoA esters, this step is performed through FadD, an inner membrane-associated acyl-CoA synthase.

The first step in the beta-oxidation cycle consists in the conversion of acyl-CoA to enoyl-CoA. The enzyme responsible for this step is fadE. It appears to be the only acyl-CoA dehydrogenase in E. coli. The beta-oxidation pathway starts under catalysis of FadE (for saturated fatty acid) and FadH (for unsaturated fatty acid) enzymes, followed by FadB and FadA enzymes.

The following steps in this cycle are: hydration, oxidation and thiolytic cleavage. They are performed by a tetrameric complex consisting of homodimers of fadB and fadA.

It is important to consider that fadH, another enoyl-CoA reductase relevant in this pathway, has been shown to have specificity for unsaturated fatty acids (UFAs), such as oleic acid [2]. This way, we can assume that there is another enoyl-CoA showing specificity for saturated fatty acids (SFAs) in E. coli. The team WHU China 2012, who worked with LCFA, didn’t observe changes in oleic acid uptake when fadE was overexpressed in E. coli. Therefore, targeting fadE in our system and demonstrating an increase in palmitic acid uptake could prove this gene’s specificity SFAs. Moreover, we could develop a system with a preference for harmful SFAs instead of beneficial UFA ones.

Taking into account the key role of these enzymes in the FA degradation metabolism we considered overexpressing them in our system. In this way, we first designed a genetic construct with pTet promoter (BBa_R0040), inducible by ATC, and a weak rbs (BBa_B0032) so as to express this genes in E.coli in a tunable expression system.

A system that could have all these genes being overexpressed at the same time would be the optimal one. Taking this into account we designed a three compatible plasmid system using three different antibiotic resistances. In this way, we could be acting and targeting all the steps in the metabolic pathway of LCFA degradation simultaneously.

Figure X | BLA BLA BLA BLA

We planned to overexpress FadL and FadD together due to its proximity in the metabolic pathway. On the other hand, FadB and FadA were also planned to be overexpressed in the same plasmid, as they form a tetrameric complex acting together.

This system will constitute a proof of concept for our probiotic. In this way antibiotic resistance is used as the selective agent. However, biocontainment remains a key issue for our team. Hence, we are planning to remove antibiotic resistance for the final design of the probiotic considering the harmful consequences of spreading antibiotic resistance.
